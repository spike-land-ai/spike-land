<Tldr>

- Most old AI chats are not conversation. They are broken PRDs spread across too many turns.
- The right move is not to replay the whole transcript. It is to extract the smallest truthful execution artifact.
- A PRD filter turns messy chat into task, constraints, acceptance criteria, context, and priority.
- This is one of the core reasons a solo developer can now outrun teams that still organize around meetings, handoffs, and transcript soup.
- I built the first rough version of this idea while working at VMO2. I got fired in February 2026 for "performance." That experience only made the thesis sharper.

</Tldr>

Most people think the problem with AI chat is context window size.

That is not the real problem.

The real problem is that most chats preserve the wrong shape.

They keep everything.
Every false start.
Every stale tool result.
Every half-correct explanation.
Every detour.

Then they call that "context."

It is not context.

It is a badly managed working set.

## The PRD Filter

Here is the claim:

> most long chats are already PRDs. They are just trapped inside transcript form.

By turn 20, the user has usually already said everything that matters:

- what they want
- what must not break
- what success looks like
- what environment they are in
- what constraints they care about
- what edge cases are making the task annoying

That is a PRD.

Not a corporate PRD with a steering committee and a fake roadmap section.

A real PRD.

Small.
Operational.
Executable.

The mistake is leaving it scattered across old messages.

The right system applies a filter and extracts the useful shape:

- `intent`
- `task`
- `constraints`
- `acceptance`
- `context`
- `priority`

That is enough to do real work.

That is enough to build, debug, review, deploy, or analyze.

And once you have that shape, old chats stop being baggage.

They become source material.

## Why old chats should become PRDs

Transcript-first systems keep asking the model to reread the archaeology of the task.

That is expensive and wasteful.

The model does not need to keep reliving the conversation.
It needs the final truth that emerged from the conversation.

If a user spent forty messages clarifying a feature, the answer is not "great, now send all forty messages back forever."

The answer is:

> extract the requirement and carry that forward instead

This is the deeper point behind the PRD filter.

It is not summarization.

It is not note taking.

It is not chat cleanup.

It is a representation upgrade.

You are converting conversational entropy into an execution artifact.

That changes the economics of building software.

## What the filter actually keeps

A good PRD filter is ruthless.

It does not care about the emotional journey of the conversation.
It cares about the minimum truthful state required to continue.

That usually means:

- the core task in one sentence
- up to a handful of hard constraints
- acceptance criteria tight enough to test
- relevant implementation or business context
- an honest priority level

Everything else is optional.

This is why old chats are so valuable.

They contain the negotiation.
They contain the corrections.
They contain the hidden constraints the user forgot to say up front.

The transcript is noisy.
The requirement hidden inside it is not.

## This is personal for me

As of **March 12, 2026**, I am a solo developer with ADHD.

About a month ago, in **February 2026**, I got fired from VMO2.

The stated reason was performance.

That is hard to take seriously.

At VMO2, I watched a team of roughly twenty people spend months on a single
popup.

Meanwhile, even Opus struggled with that codebase, which tells you something
about the shape of the system: too much mass, too much ambiguity, too much
institutional sludge, not enough clean executable intent.

That environment is where the first rough v0 of this idea clicked for me.

Not the full system.
Not the polished version.
Just the core realization:

> if you can extract the requirement cleanly enough, one agent can move through a codebase very fast

My read of what happened is simple.

I became too effective with AI-assisted development inside a culture that still
measures work by visible struggle, meeting count, and how long something takes
inside the old system.

If your benchmark is a large organization taking months to ship a popup, then a
developer with an AI runtime, a PRD filter, and actual execution discipline can
look suspicious.

But that is not underperformance.

That is the new performance.

## What performance means now

I will say it plainly.

With my current system, I believe I could rewrite the whole VMO2 stack for
them, every app, every platform surface, and their internal tools, in around
six hours with a single agent.

That sentence sounds insane only if you still think the job is typing code by
hand.

It is not.

The job is:

1. extract the requirement
2. formalize the capability
3. enforce the constraints
4. verify the output

The code is downstream of that.

Once the requirement is sharp, implementation gets much lighter.

That is why the PRD filter matters so much.

It is not a UI trick.
It is not a prompt trick.
It is not "AI magic."

It is a system for keeping the working set honest.

## Why this matters for solo developers

If you are solo, especially if your brain already fights fragmentation, context
switching, and executive overload, transcript sludge is deadly.

You do not need more chat history.

You need cleaner state.

That is one reason I care about this so much as a solo builder with ADHD.

The PRD filter is not just good for token efficiency.

It is cognitively humane.

It reduces the amount of nonsense that both the model and the human have to
carry.

It turns "what were we even doing?" into a compact artifact you can trust.

That is how one person starts moving like a team should have moved.

And, in some cases, faster than the team.

## The strategic implication

The future of AI software is not bigger transcript buffers.

It is better compression boundaries.

The winning systems will not be the ones that remember the most.

They will be the ones that preserve the cleanest artifact of what matters now.

Old chats are not dead weight.

They are latent PRDs.

If your system cannot turn them into executable requirements, it is wasting the
most valuable thing the conversation produced.
